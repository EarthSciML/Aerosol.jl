Cloud Physics

Overview

This module implements the cloud microphysics equations from Chapter 17 of Seinfeld & Pandis (2006), covering thermodynamic properties of water, the Kelvin (curvature) effect, Kأ¶hler theory for cloud droplet activation, diffusional growth of cloud droplets, adiabatic cooling and cloud formation dynamics, ice nucleation, rain formation, and aerosol scavenging by cloud droplets.

Reference: Seinfeld, J.H. and Pandis, S.N. (2006). Atmospheric Chemistry and Physics: From Air Pollution to Climate Change, 2nd Edition, John Wiley & Sons, Inc. Chapter 17: Cloud Physics.

Figure 17.1: Saturation Concentration of Water Vapor vs Temperature

The saturation concentration of water vapor over a flat water surface increases approximately exponentially with temperature. This figure shows the concentration calculated from the saturation vapor pressure (Table 17.2 polynomial) using the ideal gas law: c = pآ·M_w / (Rآ·T).

using Plots, NonlinearSolve

wp = CloudWaterProperties()
wp_sys = mtkcompile(wp)

M_w = 0.018015  # kg/mol, molecular weight of water
R = 8.314       # J/(molآ·K), universal gas constant

T_range = 263.15:1.0:303.15  # -10آ°C to 30آ°C (matching Fig. 17.1 range)
c_sat_vals = Float64[]

for T_val in T_range
    prob = NonlinearProblem(wp_sys, Dict(wp_sys.T => T_val))
    sol = solve(prob)
    p_sat = sol[wp_sys.p_sat_water]  # Pa
    # Convert pressure to concentration: c = pآ·M_w / (Rآ·T) in kg/mآ³, then to g/mآ³
    c_sat = p_sat * M_w / (R * T_val) * 1000  # g/mآ³
    push!(c_sat_vals, c_sat)
end

plot(T_range .- 273.15, c_sat_vals,
    xlabel = "Temperature (آ°C)", ylabel = "Water Vapor Concentration (g mâپ»آ³)",

    linewidth = 2, legend = :topleft,
